IDSA Syndrome-Based Guidelines
Anatomic Site → Pathogen Probability
Clinical syndrome defines the microbial universe before age, immune status, or drug selection
Respiratory
  • CAP: S. pneumoniae, atypicals (Legionella, Mycoplasma), viral
  • HAP/VAP (>48h): P. aeruginosa, MRSA, ESBL GNRs
  • Aspiration: anaerobes, mixed oral flora, S. anginosus group
  • Immunocompromised: Aspergillus, PCP, CMV pneumonitis
Urinary Tract
  • Uncomplicated cystitis: E. coli (80–85%), Staph. saprophyticus
  • Complicated/CAUTI: Klebsiella, Enterococcus, Pseudomonas
  • Urosepsis: GNRs including ESBL producers; blood cx essential
  • Males: always consider Enterococcus, prostatitis (CBP vs. ABP)
CNS
  • Community meningitis: S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis
  • Age >50 / immunocomp: add Listeria monocytogenes
  • Post-neurosurgical/shunt: S. aureus, S. epidermidis, GNRs
  • Encephalitis: HSV-1 (most common treatable), arboviral, autoimmune
Skin, Soft Tissue & Bone
  • Non-purulent cellulitis: GAS (>70%), S. aureus
  • Purulent/abscess: CA-MRSA dominant; culture pus if possible
  • Diabetic foot: polymicrobial — GPC, GNR, anaerobes
  • Osteomyelitis: S. aureus (80%), Salmonella (sickle cell)

Cunha Principle — Site First The anatomic site is not merely descriptive — it is the primary determinant of the pathogen short-list. Before considering host factors or drug selection, ask: What organisms colonize and infect this compartment under these clinical conditions?

IDSA Immunocompromised Host Guidelines
Narrowing the Pathogen Short-List
Four epidemiologic variables that stratify microbial probability — use all four simultaneously
Age
  • Neonates (<3 mo): GBS, E. coli, Listeria, HSV
  • Children: H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, RSV, Bordetella
  • Young adults: N. meningitidis, atypicals, EBV
  • >65 yrs: Listeria, GNRs, atypicals, reactivation TB ↑
Immune Defect
  • CD4 <200: PCP, MAC, Toxoplasma, Cryptococcus
  • Neutropenia (<500 ANC): Aspergillus, GNRs, Candida, VGS
  • SOT: CMV, BK virus, Nocardia, PCP
  • Asplenia: encapsulated organisms — rapid, fulminant course
Exposure / Epidemiology
  • Travel to endemic areas: Plasmodium, enteric fever, arboviruses
  • Animal contact: Brucella, Coxiella burnetii, Pasteurella
  • Soil/construction/bird droppings: Histoplasma, Blastomyces
  • Tick bite: Borrelia, Ehrlichia, Anaplasma, Babesia
Healthcare vs. Community
  • Community-onset: susceptible wild-type flora; narrow empiric preferred
  • HCAP risk criteria: prior abx ×90d, ICU >5d, wound care, dialysis
  • HAP/VAP: MDR GNRs (Pseudomonas, ESBL), MRSA coverage
  • Device-related BSI: CoNS, S. aureus, biofilm-forming organisms

Cunha Principle — Probabilistic Targeting Empiric therapy is probabilistic — you are treating the most likely pathogen(s) for this patient's epidemiologic profile, not every conceivable organism. Spectrum excess is not safety; it is unnecessary collateral damage and resistance selection pressure.

Step 03 · Pharmacology
What is the drug's spectrum?
Match spectrum to pathogen — avoid over-broad empiric coverage.

The narrowest effective agent is always preferred. Spectrum excess = collateral damage, C. difficile risk, and resistance selection.

"The best drug is the right drug — not the broadest drug."
Cunha · Antibiotic Essentials
ASHP / IDSA Stewardship Core Measures
Spectrum Matching — The Core of Antibiotic Stewardship
Calibrate coverage to the identified pathogen probability — no wider, no narrower
Narrow-Spectrum — Preferred When
  • Known susceptible organism identified on culture/susceptibility
  • Community-onset infection without MDR risk factors
  • Syndrome with single dominant pathogen (GAS pharyngitis → penicillin)
  • Targeted de-escalation from initial broad empiric therapy
  • Prevention of C. difficile, fungal superinfection, VRE selection
Broad-Spectrum — Justified When
  • Septic shock / hemodynamic instability prior to culture results
  • Known MDR organism or confirmed epidemiologic risk factors
  • Polymicrobial infection (diabetic foot, aspiration pneumonia)
  • Febrile neutropenia per institutional protocol
  • Immunocompromised host with unknown focal source

Cunha Stewardship Principle Resistance is a consequence of spectrum excess, duration excess, and dose excess — all three must be minimized. The "empiric broad → de-escalate" paradigm only works if de-escalation actually occurs. Failure to narrow at 48–72h is a stewardship failure.

ASHP Therapeutic Guidelines
Pharmacokinetics & Pharmacodynamics
Matching drug behavior to anatomic compartment and bactericidal killing mechanism
PK/PD Killing Targets
  • Time-dependent (β-lactams): %T>MIC ≥40–50% for bacteriostatic; ≥70% for bactericidal → extended infusion strategy
  • Concentration-dependent (aminoglycosides, FQs): Cmax/MIC >8–10 → once-daily high-dose preferred
  • AUC/MIC-dependent (vancomycin): AUC/MIC 400–600 (MRSA); now preferred over trough-only monitoring
  • Extended infusion β-lactams: pip-tazo 3.375g over 4h, meropenem over 3h for high-MIC organisms
Site Penetration — Key Clinical Points
  • CNS: ceftriaxone ✓ (inflamed meninges), meropenem ✓, rifampin ✓; vancomycin poor unless inflamed — add rifampin if needed
  • Lung: FQs, azithromycin — ELF:plasma >10:1; β-lactams adequate at standard doses
  • Bone: FQs ✓✓, clindamycin ✓✓, rifampin ✓✓; cefazolin adequate for MSSA osteomyelitis
  • Prostate: FQs and TMP-SMX only — most agents fail due to pH/lipid barrier; β-lactams inadequate
  • Urine: most agents concentrate in urine; azithromycin does NOT achieve therapeutic urinary levels

Cunha Principle — Penetration Equals Efficacy Spectrum without penetration is clinical failure. Choosing the drug with the correct spectrum but inadequate site penetration (e.g., vancomycin for prostatitis, azithromycin as a UTI monotherapy) will result in treatment failure regardless of in vitro susceptibility.

Renal dosing: Micromedex / Lexicomp
ACOG Antimicrobial Use in Pregnancy
Host-Specific Prescribing Constraints
Tailoring pharmacotherapy to individual physiology, safety, and drug interactions
Renal Impairment — Dose Adjustment
  • Vancomycin: AUC-guided dosing (Bayesian PK); trough-only monitoring obsolete
  • Aminoglycosides: avoid CrCl <30 unless life-saving; traditional or Hartford nomogram dosing
  • β-lactams: most require dose/interval adjustment; exceptions = ceftriaxone, nafcillin (hepatic excretion)
  • Nitrofurantoin: contraindicated CrCl <30 — no urinary concentration, inadequate levels
  • Daptomycin: q48h if CrCl <30; CPK monitoring
  • FQs (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin): dose reduction in renal failure (levofloxacin); moxifloxacin hepatic — no renal adj.
Hepatic Impairment & Pregnancy
  • Metronidazole: ↓ dose / ↑ interval in Child-Pugh C; neurotoxicity risk
  • Rifampin: potent hepatotoxin; monitor LFTs; avoid if severe baseline disease
  • Clindamycin: hepatically cleared; dose reduction in severe hepatic failure
  • Safe in pregnancy: penicillins, cephalosporins, azithromycin, clindamycin
  • Avoid pregnancy: FQs (cartilage), tetracyclines (teeth/bone, avoid >15 wks), TMP-SMX (folate 1st tri; kernicterus 3rd tri)
  • Aminoglycosides: fetal VIII-nerve toxicity — use only if life-threatening and no alternative

PRORATA Trial; NEJM PCT Meta-Analysis 2019
De-escalation Strategy at 48–72 Hours
Culture-guided narrowing + biomarker-guided duration + clinical-guided IV→PO conversion
Culture-Guided Narrowing
  • Identify organism + susceptibilities → target monotherapy where possible
  • MSSA: de-escalate from vancomycin → nafcillin, oxacillin, or cefazolin (superior outcomes)
  • Susceptible E. coli urosepsis: meropenem → ceftriaxone or ciprofloxacin (if FQ-susceptible)
  • Negative cultures + clinical improvement: reassess non-infectious etiology; consider stopping abx
  • Double antipseudomonal coverage: narrow to monotherapy once susceptibilities confirmed
Procalcitonin-Guided Duration
  • PCT <0.1 ng/mL: bacterial infection very unlikely → strongly consider discontinuation
  • PCT <0.25 ng/mL with ≥80% decrease from peak: supports discontinuation in CAP, sepsis
  • Serial PCT outperforms fixed-duration protocols: ↓ antibiotic-days, non-inferior outcomes (PRORATA trial; NEJM 2019 meta-analysis)
  • Limitations: PCT elevated in non-infective states — post-cardiac surgery, severe trauma, cardiogenic shock, ARDS
  • PCT less reliable in fungal infection, atypicals, localized infections (endocarditis, abscess)
IV-to-Oral Switch Criteria
  • Clinical improvement documented: afebrile ≥24h, hemodynamically stable, improving inflammatory markers
  • Functioning GI tract — tolerating PO, no malabsorption, no ileus
  • High bioavailability oral agents: FQs (~100%), linezolid (100%), metronidazole (>95%), TMP-SMX (90–100%), doxycycline (93%)
  • Exceptions requiring prolonged IV: S. aureus bacteremia (minimum 14d IV for MSSA/MRSA), IE, CNS infections, endovascular source
Duration Principles
  • Duration = clinical response, not calendar — shorter validated courses superior in most syndromes
  • CAP: 5 days if clinical stability achieved by day 3 (IDSA/ATS 2019); PCT-guided often permits ≤5d
  • UTI: uncomplicated 3d (TMP-SMX) or 5d (nitrofurantoin); complicated 7–14d per response
  • Cellulitis: minimum 5d; extend only if no improvement at 48–72h — reassess diagnosis
  • Sepsis (non-bacteremic, source controlled): 7d total if PCT-guided; 14d if bacteremic without IE

Can this patient receive it safely?
Renal/hepatic function, allergy status, pregnancy, and drug interactions may all contraindicate the otherwise optimal agent. The Cunha chelation phenomenon (FQs/tetracyclines + divalent cations) is a high-yield, frequently missed interaction.
