Step 01 · Foundation
What is the syndrome / site?
Identify the anatomic focus → drives pathogen probability

The anatomic compartment is the single most powerful filter for narrowing empiric coverage.

Cunha, Antibiotic Essentials, Ch. 2
🫁
Anatomic Site → Pathogen Probability
Clinical syndrome defines the microbial universe before any other variable
Respiratory
  • CAP: S. pneumoniae, atypicals, viral
  • HAP/VAP: P. aeruginosa, MRSA, GNR
  • Aspiration: anaerobes, mixed oral flora
  • Immunocompromised: Aspergillus, PCP
Urinary Tract
  • Uncomplicated: E. coli (80–85%)
  • Complicated/CAUTI: Klebsiella, Enterococcus
  • Urosepsis: GNRs incl. ESBL producers
  • Males: consider Enterococcus, prostatitis
CNS
  • Community meningitis: pneumococcus, N. meningitidis
  • Age >50/immunocomp: + Listeria
  • Post-neurosurgical: S. aureus, GNRs
  • Encephalitis: HSV-1, arboviral, autoimmune
Skin & Soft Tissue / Bone
  • Cellulitis: GAS, S. aureus (CA-MRSA)
  • Diabetic foot: polymicrobial, anaerobes
  • Osteomyelitis: S. aureus dominant (80%)
  • IV drug use: CA-MRSA, Candida

Syndrome Key Clinical Feature Refines Toward

UTI + perinephric fat strandingCT findingComplicated UTI / early abscess

Cunha Principle The anatomic site is not merely descriptive — it is the primary determinant of the pathogen short-list. Before considering host factors or drug selection, ask: What organisms colonize and infect this compartment under these clinical conditions?
2
Step 02 · Microbiology
What are the likely pathogens?
Age · immune status · exposure · healthcare vs. community onset

The pathogen list is probabilistic, not exhaustive. Focus empiric therapy on the most likely organisms.
🦠
Narrowing the Pathogen Short-List
Four epidemiologic variables that stratify microbial probability
Age
  • Neonates: GBS, E. coli, Listeria
  • Children: H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, RSV
  • Adults: standard pathogen hierarchies
  • >65 yrs: Listeria, atypicals, GNRs ↑
Immune Status
  • CD4 <200: PCP, MAC, Toxoplasma
  • Neutropenic: Aspergillus, GNRs, VGS
  • Transplant: CMV, BK virus, Nocardia
  • Splenectomy: encapsulated organisms
Exposure / Epidemiology
  • Travel: endemic mycoses, Plasmodium
  • Animal: Brucella, Coxiella, Pasteurella
  • Soil/construction: Histoplasma, Blastomyces
  • Tick: Borrelia, Ehrlichia, Babesia
Healthcare vs. Community
  • Community: susceptible wild-type flora
  • HCAP risk: prior abx, ICU >5d, dialysis
  • HAP/VAP: MDR GNRs, MRSA coverage
  • Device-related: CoNS, biofilm organisms
Pathogen Probability Hierarchy by Immune Defect
Immune Defect Mechanism Dominant Pathogens
Humoral (B-cell)↓ opsonizationEncapsulated bacteria (S. pneumo, H. flu)
Cellular (T-cell)↓ intracellular killingListeria, Toxoplasma, Cryptococcus, CMV
Neutropenia (<500)↓ phagocytosisGNRs, Aspergillus, Candida

Cunha Principle Empiric therapy is probabilistic — you are treating the most likely pathogen(s) for this patient's epidemiologic profile, not every conceivable organism. Broad coverage is not default; it must be justified by specific risk factors.
3
Step 03 · Pharmacology
What is the drug's spectrum?
Match spectrum to pathogen — avoid over-broad empiric coverage

The narrowest effective agent is always preferred. Spectrum excess = collateral damage.
💊
Spectrum Matching — The Core of Stewardship
Calibrate coverage to the identified pathogen probability — no wider
Narrow-Spectrum — When to Choose
  • Known susceptible organism on culture
  • Community-onset, no MDR risk factors
  • Syndrome with single dominant pathogen (GAS pharyngitis → PCN)
  • Targeted step-down from broad empiric
Broad-Spectrum — When Justified
  • Septic shock / high acuity before cultures
  • Known MDR organism or risk factors
  • Polymicrobial infection (diabetic foot, aspiration)
  • Febrile neutropenia protocols

Cunha Principle The "best drug" is not the broadest drug — it is the agent with optimal spectrum, PK/PD, and tolerability for this organism in this host. Resistance is driven by excess spectrum; stewardship requires matching, not maximizing, coverage.

Step 04 · PK / PD
What are the PK/PD properties?
Does the drug reach the site at adequate concentrations?

Spectrum without penetration is failure.

Cunha, Antibiotic Essentials, Ch. 8
📊
Pharmacokinetics & Pharmacodynamics
Matching drug behavior to anatomic compartment and killing mechanism
PK/PD Killing Targets
  • Time-dependent: β-lactams — %T>MIC ≥40–50%
  • Concentration-dependent: aminoglycosides, FQs — Cmax/MIC >8–10
  • AUC-dependent: vancomycin — AUC/MIC 400–600
  • Extended infusion: pip-tazo, meropenem for high MIC
Site Penetration
  • CNS: ceftriaxone, meropenem, rifampin ✓
  • CNS: vancomycin (poor unless inflamed)
  • Lung: FQs, azithromycin (tissue:plasma >10:1)
  • Bone: FQs, clindamycin, rifampin ✓
  • Prostate: only FQs, TMP-SMX, doxycycline
  • Urine: most abx concentrate; exception = azithromycin
Intracellular Pathogens
  • Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila
  • Require intracellular active agents
  • Macrolides, FQs, doxycycline ✓
  • β-lactams: ineffective (extracellular only)
Protein Binding & Volume
  • High VD (FQs, azithromycin): excellent tissue
  • Low VD (aminoglycosides): poor intracellular
  • High PB (>90%): ↓ free drug activity
  • Hypoalbuminemia: alters free fraction for many abx

5
Step 05 · Host Factors
What is the host?
Renal · hepatic function · allergies · pregnancy · drug interactions

The correct drug for the pathogen may be the wrong drug for the patient.
🧬
Host-Specific Prescribing Constraints
Tailoring pharmacotherapy to individual patient physiology and safety
Renal Impairment
  • Vancomycin: AUC-guided dosing; trough-only obsolete
  • Aminoglycosides: avoid if eGFR <30 unless no alternative
  • β-lactams: dose-adjust many (except ceftriaxone, nafcillin)
  • Nitrofurantoin: contraindicated CrCl <30 (no urinary concentration)
  • Daptomycin: q48h if CrCl <30
Hepatic Impairment
  • Metronidazole: ↓ dose in severe hepatic disease
  • Rifampin: use caution; hepatotoxic
  • Clindamycin: hepatically metabolized; consider dose reduction
  • Azithromycin: caution Child-Pugh C
  • Most β-lactams: safe (renal excretion dominant)
Allergy Assessment
  • PCN allergy: 1–2% true cross-reactivity with cephalosporins (R1 side chain)
  • Anaphylaxis/urticaria: prefer non-β-lactam or ID/allergy referral
  • Maculopapular rash: likely not IgE-mediated; lower risk
  • Sulfonamide "allergy": ~90% non-cross reactive with TMP-SMX in practice
Pregnancy & Lactation
  • Safe: penicillins, cephalosporins, azithromycin, clindamycin
  • Avoid: FQs (cartilage), tetracyclines (tooth discoloration), metronidazole 1st trimester (relative)
  • TMP-SMX: folate antagonism 1st tri; kernicterus 3rd tri
  • Aminoglycosides: ototoxicity risk; use only if life-saving

Step 06 · Stewardship
De-escalate at 48–72 h
Narrow based on culture data · clinical improvement · procalcitonin

Empiric broad coverage is a bridge, not a destination. Failing to de-escalate is a stewardship failure.
📉
De-escalation Strategy at 48–72 Hours
Antibiotic stewardship completes the Cunha loop — culture + clinical + biomarker-guided narrowing
Culture-Guided Narrowing
  • Identify organism + susceptibilities → target monotherapy if possible
  • MSSA: de-escalate from vancomycin to nafcillin/cefazolin
  • Susceptible E. coli: narrow from meropenem to ceftriaxone
  • Negative cultures in clinical improver → reconsider viral/non-infective etiology
Biomarker-Guided: Procalcitonin
  • PCT <0.1 ng/mL: bacterial infection very unlikely → discontinue
  • PCT decrease ≥80% from peak: supports discontinuation
  • Serial PCT outperforms fixed-duration in CAP, sepsis
  • Limitations: PCT elevated in non-infective states (trauma, post-op, cardiogenic shock)
Clinical Improvement Criteria
  • Defervescence, improving WBC trend
  • Hemodynamic stabilization off vasopressors
  • Improving end-organ function markers
  • Resolution of source (abscess drained, line removed)
  • Tolerating PO → IV-to-PO switch if bioavailable agent available
IV-to-Oral Switch Criteria
  • Clinical improvement, afebrile ≥24h
  • Functioning GI tract (tolerating PO)
  • High-bioavailability oral agent exists: FQs, linezolid, metronidazole, TMP-SMX, doxycycline (~100%)
  • No bacteremia with certain organisms (S. aureus: typically 14d IV minimum)

Cunha Stewardship Principle Duration of therapy is determined by clinical response, not arbitrary fixed courses. The stewardship imperative is to de-escalate as soon as it is clinically safe — resistance is bred by unnecessary spectrum, dose, and duration.
