Step 01 · Foundation
What is the syndrome / site?
Identify the anatomic focus → drives pathogen probability

The anatomic compartment is the single most powerful filter for narrowing empiric coverage.

Cunha, Antibiotic Essentials, Ch. 2
🫁
Anatomic Site → Pathogen Probability
Clinical syndrome defines the microbial universe before any other variable
Respiratory
  • CAP: S. pneumoniae, atypicals, viral
  • HAP/VAP: P. aeruginosa, MRSA, GNR
  • Aspiration: anaerobes, mixed oral flora
  • Immunocompromised: Aspergillus, PCP
Urinary Tract
  • Uncomplicated: E. coli (80–85%)
  • Complicated/CAUTI: Klebsiella, Enterococcus
  • Urosepsis: GNRs incl. ESBL producers
  • Males: consider Enterococcus, prostatitis
CNS
  • Community meningitis: pneumococcus, N. meningitidis
  • Age >50/immunocomp: + Listeria
  • Post-neurosurgical: S. aureus, GNRs
  • Encephalitis: HSV-1, arboviral, autoimmune
Skin & Soft Tissue / Bone
  • Cellulitis: GAS, S. aureus (CA-MRSA)
  • Diabetic foot: polymicrobial, anaerobes
  • Osteomyelitis: S. aureus dominant (80%)
  • IV drug use: CA-MRSA, Candida

Cunha Principle The anatomic site is not merely descriptive — it is the primary determinant of the pathogen short-list. Before considering host factors or drug selection, ask: What organisms colonize and infect this compartment under these clinical conditions?

Step 02 · Microbiology
What are the likely pathogens?
Age · immune status · exposure · healthcare vs. community onset

The pathogen list is probabilistic, not exhaustive. Focus empiric therapy on the most likely organisms.
🦠
Narrowing the Pathogen Short-List
Four epidemiologic variables that stratify microbial probability
Age
  • Neonates: GBS, E. coli, Listeria
  • Children: H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, RSV
  • Adults: standard pathogen hierarchies
  • >65 yrs: Listeria, atypicals, GNRs ↑
Immune Status
  • CD4 <200: PCP, MAC, Toxoplasma
  • Neutropenic: Aspergillus, GNRs, VGS
  • Transplant: CMV, BK virus, Nocardia
  • Splenectomy: encapsulated organisms
Exposure / Epidemiology
  • Travel: endemic mycoses, Plasmodium
  • Animal: Brucella, Coxiella, Pasteurella
  • Soil/construction: Histoplasma, Blastomyces
  • Tick: Borrelia, Ehrlichia, Babesia
Healthcare vs. Community
  • Community: susceptible wild-type flora
  • HCAP risk: prior abx, ICU >5d, dialysis
  • HAP/VAP: MDR GNRs, MRSA coverage
  • Device-related: CoNS, biofilm organisms

Cunha Principle Empiric therapy is probabilistic — you are treating the most likely pathogen(s) for this patient's epidemiologic profile, not every conceivable organism. Broad coverage is not default; it must be justified by specific risk factors.

Step 03 · Pharmacology
What is the drug's spectrum?
Match spectrum to pathogen — avoid over-broad empiric coverage

The narrowest effective agent is always preferred. Spectrum excess = collateral damage.
💊
Spectrum Matching — The Core of Stewardship
Calibrate coverage to the identified pathogen probability — no wider
Narrow-Spectrum — When to Choose
  • Known susceptible organism on culture
  • Community-onset, no MDR risk factors
  • Syndrome with single dominant pathogen (GAS pharyngitis → PCN)
  • Targeted step-down from broad empiric
Broad-Spectrum — When Justified
  • Septic shock / high acuity before cultures
  • Known MDR organism or risk factors
  • Polymicrobial infection (diabetic foot, aspiration)
  • Febrile neutropenia protocols

Step 04 · PK / PD
What are the PK/PD properties?
Does the drug reach the site at adequate concentrations?

Spectrum without penetration is failure.

Cunha, Antibiotic Essentials, Ch. 8
📊
Pharmacokinetics & Pharmacodynamics
Matching drug behavior to anatomic compartment and killing mechanism
PK/PD Killing Targets
  • Time-dependent: β-lactams — %T>MIC ≥40–50%
  • Concentration-dependent: aminoglycosides, FQs — Cmax/MIC >8–10
  • AUC-dependent: vancomycin — AUC/MIC 400–600
  • Extended infusion: pip-tazo, meropenem for high MIC
Site Penetration
  • CNS: ceftriaxone, meropenem, rifampin ✓
  • CNS: vancomycin (poor unless inflamed)
  • Lung: FQs, azithromycin (tissue:plasma >10:1)
  • Bone: FQs, clindamycin, rifampin ✓
  • Prostate: only FQs, TMP-SMX, doxycycline
  • Urine: most abx concentrate; exception = azithromycin
Intracellular Pathogens
  • Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila
  • Require intracellular active agents
  • Macrolides, FQs, doxycycline ✓
  • β-lactams: ineffective (extracellular only)
Protein Binding & Volume
  • High VD (FQs, azithromycin): excellent tissue
  • Low VD (aminoglycosides): poor intracellular
  • High PB (>90%): ↓ free drug activity
  • Hypoalbuminemia: alters free fraction for many abx

Step 05 · Host Factors
What is the host?
Renal · hepatic function · allergies · pregnancy · drug interactions

The correct drug for the pathogen may be the wrong drug for the patient.
🧬
Host-Specific Prescribing Constraints
Tailoring pharmacotherapy to individual patient physiology and safety
Renal Impairment
  • Vancomycin: AUC-guided dosing; trough-only obsolete
  • Aminoglycosides: avoid if eGFR <30 unless no alternative
  • β-lactams: dose-adjust many (except ceftriaxone, nafcillin)
  • Nitrofurantoin: contraindicated CrCl <30 (no urinary concentration)
  • Daptomycin: q48h if CrCl <30
Hepatic Impairment
  • Metronidazole: ↓ dose in severe hepatic disease
  • Rifampin: use caution; hepatotoxic
  • Clindamycin: hepatically metabolized; consider dose reduction
  • Azithromycin: caution Child-Pugh C
  • Most β-lactams: safe (renal excretion dominant)
Allergy Assessment
  • PCN allergy: 1–2% true cross-reactivity with cephalosporins (R1 side chain)
  • Anaphylaxis/urticaria: prefer non-β-lactam or ID/allergy referral
  • Maculopapular rash: likely not IgE-mediated; lower risk
  • Sulfonamide "allergy": ~90% non-cross reactive with TMP-SMX in practice
Pregnancy & Lactation
  • Safe: penicillins, cephalosporins, azithromycin, clindamycin
  • Avoid: FQs (cartilage), tetracyclines (tooth discoloration), metronidazole 1st trimester (relative)
  • TMP-SMX: folate antagonism 1st tri; kernicterus 3rd tri
  • Aminoglycosides: ototoxicity risk; use only if life-saving

Antibiotic Drug Interactions — What Patients Don't Volunteer
OTC minerals, supplements, and common medications that silently sabotage antibiotic efficacy
Step 5 Extension · Host Factors
🧲
The Chelation Phenomenon — Cunha
Divalent & Trivalent Cations Bind Antibiotics in the GI Tract
Fe²⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺, and Al³⁺ form insoluble complexes with fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines in the gut lumen, reducing oral bioavailability by 50–90%. The drug is present in the capsule but never absorbed. The patient takes the antibiotic — it just doesn't work.
Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin)
Chelation with any divalent/trivalent cation → ciprofloxacin bioavailability drops from ~70% to <10% if taken simultaneously with antacids or multivitamins. Levofloxacin similarly affected. Most under-recognized cause of oral FQ treatment failure.
Tetracyclines (Doxycycline, Minocycline)
Same chelation mechanism. Doxycycline absorption ↓ by ~80–85% with iron. Dairy (Ca²⁺) reduces absorption significantly but less than iron supplements. Minocycline slightly less susceptible to dairy than doxycycline but still affected by supplement-level cation doses.
Separation Rule — When to Take Antibiotics Relative to Cations
→ FQs: Take antibiotic ≥2h before or ≥6h after cation-containing product
→ Tetracyclines: Take ≥2h before or ≥4h after; avoid dairy within 1h of doxycycline dose
→ IV route bypasses entirely — chelation is exclusively a PO bioavailability issue
→ Moxifloxacin (PO): same rule applies despite good bioavailability at baseline
🛒
OTC Products Patients Forget to Mention
The History Gap Patients reliably report prescription medications but routinely omit vitamins, supplements, and antacids — often because they don't consider them "real medicine." Always ask specifically: "Do you take any vitamins, minerals, antacids, or herbal supplements?"
Multivitamins
Centrum, One-A-Day, Nature Made contain Fe²⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺ — all four chelating cations in one tablet. Taken daily, often at breakfast with the antibiotic.
Calcium Supplements
Caltrate, Os-Cal, Viactiv, Citracal. Often taken twice daily. TUMS used as a Ca supplement adds Ca²⁺ 3–4× daily. Especially common in older women.
Iron Supplements
Slow FE, FeroSul, ferrous sulfate 325mg. Most potent chelator of the group. 1 tablet reduces ciprofloxacin bioavailability to near zero if taken simultaneously.
Antacids
Maalox, Mylanta, Gaviscon, Rolaids contain Mg²⁺/Al³⁺/Ca²⁺. Taken PRN for heartburn — patient may not realize it's an issue.
Zinc Supplements
Zinc lozenges (Cold-EEZE, Zicam), zinc gluconate 50mg tabs. Popular OTC immune supplements — Zn²⁺ is a potent chelator.
Magnesium
Mg supplements (for sleep, constipation, muscle cramps — Nature Calm, MOM) add Mg²⁺. Also PPIs → hypomagnesemia paradox: more Mg supplementation prescribed.
Prenatal Vitamins
Highest chelator load: Fe²⁺ ~27mg + Ca²⁺ + Zn²⁺ + Mg²⁺ all in one capsule. Critical to counsel pregnant patients receiving doxycycline (though doxy itself avoided in pregnancy).
Dairy Foods
Milk, yogurt, cheese contain Ca²⁺. Less potent than supplements but clinically relevant for tetracyclines if consumed within 1h of dose. FQ interaction with dairy is minor.
💊
Beyond Chelation — CYP, QTc & Serotonin
Rifampin — Potent CYP3A4/2C9 Inducer
↓ levels of warfarin (may need 2–3× dose increase; INR monitoring q3–5d at initiation), calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus — transplant rejection risk), combined oral contraceptives (OCP failure), HIV ARVs (PIs, NNRTIs), methadone, many antiseizure medications. Review entire medication list before starting rifampin.
Metronidazole — CYP2C9 Inhibition + Disulfiram Effect
Inhibits CYP2C9 → ↑ warfarin effect (INR may double); check INR within 3–5d of starting. Disulfiram-like reaction with ethanol (even mouthwash, liquid medications, vanilla extract): flushing, tachycardia, nausea, vomiting. Counsel patients explicitly — "no alcohol for the duration and 48h after the last dose."
FQs & Azithromycin — QTc Prolongation
Additive QTc risk with: antipsychotics (haloperidol, quetiapine, ziprasidone), antihistamines (hydroxyzine, diphenhydramine — OTC sleep aids and Benadryl), ondansetron, tricyclic antidepressants, amiodarone. Check baseline QTc; if >470ms (F) / >450ms (M): reconsider or monitor. Azithromycin + quetiapine is a common high-risk pairing.
Linezolid — MAO Inhibition & Serotonin Syndrome
Weak, reversible MAO inhibitor. Risk with: SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine — very common), SNRIs, tramadol, OTC dextromethorphan (DXM) in cough suppressants (Robitussin DM, NyQuil), meperidine, triptans, St. John's Wort. DXM interaction is frequently missed — patients don't consider cough syrup a drug interaction risk. Symptoms: hyperthermia, clonus, agitation, diaphoresis.
St. John's Wort — CYP3A4 Inducer (OTC Herbal)
Hypericum perforatum: widely used OTC for depression/anxiety. Induces CYP3A4 similarly to rifampin — ↓ levels of antiretrovirals, cyclosporine, warfarin, oral contraceptives. Patients virtually never mention herbal supplements unless directly asked. Also serotonergic → additive risk with linezolid.
The 5-Second Screening Question Before prescribing any oral FQ, tetracycline, linezolid, or rifampin-based regimen, ask: "Do you take any vitamins, minerals, antacids, herbal supplements, or anything from a health food store?" — and specifically ask about multivitamins, calcium, iron, and cough medicine by name. Most patients will not connect these to antibiotic therapy without explicit prompting.

Step 06 · Stewardship
De-escalate at 48–72 h
Narrow based on culture data · clinical improvement · procalcitonin

Empiric broad coverage is a bridge, not a destination. Failing to de-escalate is a stewardship failure.
📉
De-escalation Strategy at 48–72 Hours
Antibiotic stewardship completes the Cunha loop — culture + clinical + biomarker-guided narrowing
Culture-Guided Narrowing
  • Identify organism + susceptibilities → target monotherapy if possible
  • MSSA: de-escalate from vancomycin to nafcillin/cefazolin
  • Susceptible E. coli: narrow from meropenem to ceftriaxone
  • Negative cultures in clinical improver → reconsider viral/non-infective etiology
Biomarker-Guided: Procalcitonin
  • PCT <0.1 ng/mL: bacterial infection very unlikely → discontinue
  • PCT decrease ≥80% from peak: supports discontinuation
  • Serial PCT outperforms fixed-duration in CAP, sepsis
  • Limitations: PCT elevated in non-infective states (trauma, post-op, cardiogenic shock)
Clinical Improvement Criteria
  • Defervescence, improving WBC trend
  • Hemodynamic stabilization off vasopressors
  • Improving end-organ function markers
  • Resolution of source (abscess drained, line removed)
  • Tolerating PO → IV-to-PO switch if bioavailable agent available
IV-to-Oral Switch Criteria
  • Clinical improvement, afebrile ≥24h
  • Functioning GI tract (tolerating PO)
  • High-bioavailability oral agent exists: FQs, linezolid, metronidazole, TMP-SMX, doxycycline (~100%)
  • No bacteremia with certain organisms (S. aureus: typically 14d IV minimum)
De-escalation Decision Matrix
Situation Action Evidence Base
Positive culture, susceptible isolateTarget monotherapy; discontinue unnecessary agentsIDSA guidelines; Cunha Antibiotic Essentials
Negative cultures + improving clinicallyShorten course or discontinue; consider viral etiologyPRORATA trial; PCT algorithms

Cunha Stewardship Principle Duration of therapy is determined by clinical response, not arbitrary fixed courses. The stewardship imperative is to de-escalate as soon as it is clinically safe — resistance is bred by unnecessary spectrum, dose, and duration.

Respiratory — Case 1 of 2
Community-Acquired Pneumonia · Atypical Pattern (Legionella)
CAP → VAP
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
52-year-old woman, previously healthy. Three-day illness: fever 39.6 °C, non-productive cough, progressive confusion, Na 128 mEq/L. Vitals: HR 82 (relative bradycardia for temp), SpO₂ 91% RA. CXR: LLL consolidation. No recent hospitalization; no antibiotics ×90d.
Key Clinical Cues Driving Pathogen Selection
①
Relative bradycardia — temp–pulse dissociation at 39.6 °C with HR 82; hallmark of atypical intracellular pathogens (Faget sign)
②
Hyponatremia — extrapulmonary manifestation strongly associated with Legionella pneumophila serogroup 1
③
Mental status change — extrapulmonary feature; seen in up to 50% of Legionella CAP, unusual for pneumococcal CAP
④
Community-onset, no MDR risk — atypical pattern + no healthcare exposures excludes standard CAP empiric monotherapy with β-lactam
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Lung — community-acquired lower lobe pneumonia; no healthcare exposures → CAP framework; lower lobe consolidation consistent with Legionella
Pathogen
Temp–pulse dissociation + hyponatremia + confusion → Legionella pneumophila (atypical, obligate intracellular). Mycoplasma and Chlamydophila possible but lack this extrapulmonary signature cluster.
Spectrum
β-lactam monotherapy insufficient — no intracellular activity. Requires FQ or macrolide. Levofloxacin preferred over azithromycin for severe/ICU Legionella (superior bactericidal activity in vitro and clinically).
PK / PD
Levofloxacin: ELF:plasma >10:1; excellent macrophage penetration where Legionella replicates intracellularly. Azithromycin: high tissue ratio but bacteriostatic — avoid if septic or ICU presentation.
Host
Healthy host; check baseline QTc (if >500ms → switch to azithromycin). No renal dose adjustment needed. Order urinary Legionella antigen (serogroup 1; sensitivity ~80%, specificity >99%).
De-escalate
Levofloxacin 750mg PO/IV × 5 days. PCT at 48h — if ↓ ≥80% and clinically improving, complete 5d course and stop. Urinary antigen positive confirms Legionella; no broadening needed.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If One Variable Changes →
Same presentation — but CD4 count returns at 72 cells/μL
Step 2 (Pathogen) shifts entirely · immune defect overrides epidemiologic atypical pattern
At CD4 <200, PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii) becomes highest priority, followed by CMV pneumonitis, Cryptococcus, and endemic fungi.

Actions: Obtain BAL urgently for Pneumocystis DFA/PCR, fungal cultures, CMV PCR. Start empiric TMP-SMX IV immediately if bilateral infiltrates + CD4 <200. Add Legionella antigen — it remains possible but is no longer the primary hypothesis.

Levofloxacin alone is not sufficient empiric therapy in this host.

Respiratory — Case 2 of 2
Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia · MDR Risk, Prior Antibiotic Exposure
CAP → VAP
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
67-year-old man, ICU day 8 post-sigmoid colectomy. Intubated. New fever 38.8 °C, increased purulent secretions, new RLL infiltrate on CXR, WBC 18.4 K with left shift. CPIS 7. Pip-tazo × 3d for surgical prophylaxis (completed day 3). Blood cultures × 2 and BAL drawn before antibiotic change.
Key Clinical Cues Driving Pathogen Selection
①
HAP/VAP, ICU >5 days — healthcare-acquired flora replaces community organisms; MDR GNRs and MRSA must be covered empirically
②
Prior pip-tazo × 3d — recent β-lactam/BLI exposure creates selective pressure for resistant GNRs (ESBL, Pseudomonas with acquired resistance)
③
Post-abdominal surgery — aspiration risk, gut flora inoculation; ESBL GNRs from enteric source are prominent concerns
④
ICU >5d — independent MRSA risk factor; add vancomycin empirically until MRSA nasal swab or cultures return negative
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Lung — VAP; intubated >48h, ICU >5d → healthcare-acquired pathogen hierarchy; community CAP organisms are not the relevant differential
Pathogen
Prior pip-tazo + ICU + post-abdominal surgery → P. aeruginosa, ESBL Klebsiella/Enterobacter, MRSA. Both GNR and GPC empiric coverage required.
Spectrum
Cefepime or pip-tazo (anti-pseudomonal) + vancomycin (MRSA). Consider meropenem upfront if institutional ESBL prevalence is high or prior ESBL isolates in this patient.
PK / PD
Extended infusion pip-tazo 3.375g q8h over 4h (or cefepime 2g q8h over 4h) maximizes %T>MIC for Pseudomonas. Vancomycin: AUC/MIC 400–600 target via Bayesian dosing; load 25–30mg/kg × 1.
Host
Post-op ICU: check SCr q12–24h; ↓ albumin alters free drug fractions; review sedation/analgesia for DDIs; MRSA nares swab (high NPV — if negative, MRSA coverage can stop at 48h).
De-escalate
BAL cx at 48h: susceptible GNR only → narrow to ceftriaxone; MRSA not confirmed → d/c vancomycin; 7–8d total if clinical response (PCT-guided). Do not continue double GNR coverage once susceptibilities known.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If Culture Results Change →
BAL grows ESBL-producing Klebsiella pneumoniae
Step 3 (Spectrum) must change · ESBL invalidates both cefepime and pip-tazo
ESBL producers hydrolyze all cephalosporins and pip-tazo — in vitro susceptibility does not translate to clinical efficacy for serious ESBL infections (inoculum effect, AUC failure at standard doses).

Switch immediately to meropenem 1g q8h IV (or 2g q8h over 3h extended infusion if MIC ≥2 mg/L). Discontinue vancomycin if no MRSA confirmed.

Continuing cefepime because it "looks susceptible" on the report is a common and consequential error for ESBL infections.

Urinary Tract — Case 1 of 2
Uncomplicated Cystitis · Healthy Young Woman, Community-Onset
Cystitis → Urosepsis
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
24-year-old woman, no comorbidities, not pregnant. Two-day dysuria, urinary frequency, and suprapubic pressure. Afebrile. No CVA tenderness. UA: pyuria (WBC >10/hpf), bacteriuria 3+, nitrites positive. No recent antibiotics, no healthcare contact, no urologic abnormalities. Local E. coli TMP-SMX resistance: 14%.
Key Clinical Cues — Why This Stays Narrow
①
Afebrile, no CVA tenderness — lower tract only; upper tract involvement would require longer course and possible IV therapy
②
No MDR risk factors — community-onset, no prior antibiotics, no healthcare exposures → susceptible wild-type E. coli overwhelmingly likely
③
Local TMP-SMX resistance 14% — below the 20% empiric threshold; TMP-SMX remains reliable as first-line therapy
④
No culture needed — IDSA: urine culture not required before treating uncomplicated cystitis in otherwise healthy non-pregnant women
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Lower urinary tract — uncomplicated cystitis; no systemic signs, no upper tract involvement; oral outpatient therapy appropriate
Pathogen
E. coli (80–85%), Staph. saprophyticus (young sexually active women, 5–15%). Community-onset, no MDR risk → susceptible wild-type organisms highly probable.
Spectrum
Narrow: TMP-SMX DS (local resistance <20%) or nitrofurantoin macrocrystals. FQ not first-line — reserve for complicated UTI/pyelonephritis (IDSA stewardship position 2011).
PK / PD
TMP-SMX achieves high urinary bactericidal concentrations; 90%+ oral bioavailability. Nitrofurantoin concentrates in urine — does not achieve therapeutic plasma or renal parenchymal levels; unsuitable for pyelonephritis.
Host
Healthy, not pregnant, normal renal function (nitrofurantoin requires CrCl >30). No drug interactions. Sulfonamide allergy? → nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin 3g × 1 dose.
De-escalate
TMP-SMX DS × 3 days; nitrofurantoin 100mg ER BID × 5 days. No test-of-cure UA required if symptoms resolve. No follow-up culture needed.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If One Variable Changes →
Same presentation — patient is 14 weeks pregnant
Step 5 (Host) overrides Steps 3 & 6 · pregnancy contraindications cascade through all first-choice agents
TMP-SMX: Folate antagonism → avoid 1st trimester; neonatal kernicterus risk in 3rd trimester.

Nitrofurantoin: Avoid at term / 3rd trimester (neonatal hemolytic anemia risk).

FQs: Avoid throughout pregnancy (cartilage toxicity).

Safe choice: cephalexin 500mg TID × 7 days. In pregnancy, all bacteriuria (including asymptomatic) must be treated — untreated bacteriuria → 25–30% pyelonephritis risk + preterm labor. Obtain culture; test-of-cure required at 1–2 weeks post-treatment.

Urinary Tract — Case 2 of 2
Urosepsis · Nursing Home Resident, Recurrent UTIs, Bacteremic
Cystitis → Urosepsis
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
72-year-old woman, T2DM, nursing home resident. Recurrent UTIs (3rd episode in 6 months). Found altered (A&O × 3 now confused). T 39.1 °C, HR 118, BP 88/56, RR 20. UA: WBC >100/hpf, bacteria 4+, gross pyuria. Foley catheter × 3 weeks (now removed). Prior aminopenicillin completed 6 weeks ago. Blood cultures × 2 drawn. Renal US ordered.
Key Clinical Cues — Why Broad Empiric Coverage Is Justified
①
Sepsis criteria met (HR 118, BP 88/56, T 39.1, AMS) — bacteremia likely; blood cultures before antibiotics, then do not delay empiric therapy
②
NH + T2DM + prior aminopenicillin + Foley — four independent risk factors for ESBL-producing GNRs and Enterococcus
③
Recurrent UTIs × 3 episodes — prior antibiotic courses drive progressive resistance; assume MDR organisms until susceptibilities return
④
AMS in elderly NH patient — altered mental status may be the only presenting UTI symptom; always consider sepsis source in confused NH residents
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Upper UTI + bacteremia — urosepsis. STAT renal US to exclude obstruction (infected obstructed kidney = urologic/IR emergency requiring urgent drainage).
Pathogen
NH + DM + prior abx + Foley → ESBL E. coli/Klebsiella, Enterococcus faecalis/faecium, Pseudomonas possible. Cephalosporins cannot cover ESBL or Enterococcus.
Spectrum
Meropenem (ESBL + broad GNR) ± vancomycin if Enterococcus risk (healthcare device, indwelling Foley). Do not use cephalosporin or FQ empirically — ESBL resistance anticipated.
PK / PD
IV only in septic shock — PO route inappropriate until hemodynamically stable. Meropenem achieves therapeutic urinary + plasma concentrations. IV-to-PO transition only after blood cultures cleared and BP stable.
Host
Urgently check eGFR: elderly + T2DM nephropathy → meropenem dose-adjust (eGFR 10–25: 500mg q12h). Vancomycin: load 25–30mg/kg × 1, then AUC-guided. Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (30mL/kg crystalloid, reassess).
De-escalate
Cx at 48h: if ESBL E. coli susceptible to ceftriaxone (tricky — confirm with ID/pharmacy) → narrow; if susceptible to FQ → IV→PO switch once stable. Total 14d for bacteremic UTI; duration from first negative blood culture.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If Culture Results Change →
Blood and urine cultures grow Enterococcus faecium — VRE confirmed
Step 3 (Spectrum) collapses · VRE is resistant to β-lactams, aminoglycosides, and vancomycin by definition
Meropenem has no Enterococcus activity. Vancomycin is inactive against VRE by definition. Both must be changed.

First-line: Linezolid 600mg IV q12h — excellent VRE faecium activity; 100% oral bioavailability enables early IV→PO switch once stable.

Alternative: Daptomycin 8–10mg/kg q24h (high-dose for serious VRE; dose-adjust for eGFR <30 → q48h).

Contact precautions; VRE screen protocol. ID consult. Minimum 14d from first negative blood culture.

Community → Device
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
21-year-old man, college student, previously healthy. 12-hour acute illness: sudden-onset severe headache ("worst of life"), fever 39.6 °C, nuchal rigidity, photophobia. GCS 14 (confused). Non-blanching petechiae on trunk and bilateral lower extremities. CT head: no mass lesion. LP planned — empiric treatment started immediately before LP result.
Key Clinical Cues — Why This Cannot Wait
①
Non-blanching petechiae — classic meningococcemia pattern; every hour without antibiotics increases mortality approximately 10%
②
Age 21 + college dormitory — N. meningitidis serogroup B/C highest probability; close contacts require chemoprophylaxis within 24h
③
Empiric before LP — do not delay antibiotics for LP; give treatment first, then LP within 30–60 min. CSF cultures remain positive for hours after first dose.
④
Dexamethasone timing — must be given with or before first antibiotic dose; neurologic benefit is lost if given afterward
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
CNS / meninges — bacterial meningitis; petechiae pattern strongly suggests meningococcemia; treat as meningococcal emergency until proven otherwise
Pathogen
Age 21 + community + petechiae → N. meningitidis most likely; S. pneumoniae also possible. Vancomycin added until pneumococcal susceptibility confirmed (covers PCN-R strains).
Spectrum
Ceftriaxone 2g q12h (meningeal penetration with inflamed BBB) + vancomycin (PCN-R pneumococcus coverage) + dexamethasone 0.15mg/kg q6h × 4d concurrent with first dose.
PK / PD
Ceftriaxone: bactericidal CSF concentrations when meninges inflamed. Dexamethasone ↓ TNF-α/IL-1β → ↓ hearing loss (NNT ~8), ↓ unfavorable neurologic outcomes. Does not impair ceftriaxone penetration at standard doses.
Host
Young, healthy; confirm β-lactam allergy history. Public health notification required for suspected meningococcal disease. Household/intimate contacts → rifampin 600mg BID × 2d or ciprofloxacin 500mg PO × 1 dose within 24h.
De-escalate
CSF cx: N. meningitidis PCN-susceptible → de-escalate to PCN G 4MU q4h × 7d; discontinue vancomycin. Meningococcal meningitis: 7-day total course. Complete 4-day dexamethasone regardless.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If One Variable Changes →
Age 68, prednisone 20mg/day × 8 months for PMR — same meningitis presentation
Step 2 adds Listeria monocytogenes · Step 3 must add ampicillin · ceftriaxone has zero Listeria activity
Chronic corticosteroids suppress T-cell immunity → intracellular pathogens emerge. Listeria monocytogenes is a serious contender at age >50 or with cell-mediated immune defect.

Add: ampicillin 2g IV q4h to ceftriaxone + vancomycin. Ceftriaxone does not cover Listeria — never assume cephalosporins cover it.

PCN anaphylaxis → TMP-SMX 5mg/kg IV q6–12h substitutes for ampicillin against Listeria.

Dexamethasone: Give with or before first antibiotic dose; some ID authorities give the first antibiotic concurrently rather than after dex to preserve CSF penetration — evidence is not definitive.

CNS Infection — Case 2 of 2
Post-Neurosurgical Meningitis · VP Shunt Infection, Device-Associated
Community → Device
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
58-year-old man, s/p VP shunt revision 5 days prior for normal-pressure hydrocephalus. New fever 38.7 °C, progressive headache, declining mental status (GCS 13). LP via shunt reservoir: WBC 840/μL (88% PMN), glucose 22 mg/dL (serum 110), protein 184 mg/dL. Gram stain: Gram-positive cocci in clusters (preliminary). Blood cultures × 2 drawn.
Key Clinical Cues — Device Changes the Pathogen Set
①
VP shunt + recent revision — S. epidermidis (CoNS) is the most common shunt pathogen (40–50%); biofilm formation is universal and renders in-situ cure essentially impossible
②
GPC on Gram stain — narrows to staphylococci; both CoNS and MRSA must be covered empirically with vancomycin
③
Shunt removal is mandatory — hardware must be externalized or removed before antibiotics can sterilize CSF; source control is non-negotiable
④
CSF glucose 22 vs. serum 110 — severe bacterial meningitis pattern; high bacterial burden; aggressive treatment required without delay
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
CSF / CNS — device-associated healthcare-acquired meningitis. Source control = shunt externalization or removal is non-negotiable. Antibiotics alone cannot sterilize biofilm.
Pathogen
VP shunt + GPC clusters on Gram stain → S. epidermidis (CoNS, most common) or MRSA; GNRs also possible post-neurosurgically (cover with second agent).
Spectrum
Vancomycin (CoNS/MRSA) + cefepime or meropenem (GNR coverage). Add rifampin 600mg PO daily for CoNS biofilm activity once organism identified — never as monotherapy.
PK / PD
Vancomycin CSF penetration poor even with inflammation; if failing → intrathecal vancomycin 5–20mg/day (neurosurgery + ID decision). Rifampin: excellent CNS penetration. Cefepime crosses BBB at standard doses.
Host
Check renal function urgently (vancomycin nephrotoxicity). Rifampin: potent CYP3A4/2C9/2C19 inducer — review all concurrent medications before starting. Neurosurgery required for hardware management.
De-escalate
Culture: S. epidermidis rifampin-susceptible → vancomycin + rifampin combination; 10–14d IV after shunt removal. Repeat LP to confirm CSF sterilization before considering shunt re-implantation.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If Culture Returns a Worst-Case Organism →
CSF culture: carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter baumannii (CRAB)
Steps 3 & 4 collapse · all standard CNS-active agents are ineffective; salvage therapy required
Meropenem, cefepime, and vancomycin all inactive against CRAB. This is a therapeutic crisis.

Salvage options:
• Polymyxin B or colistin IV — poor intrinsic CSF penetration; systemic therapy may be insufficient alone.
• Intraventricular colistin 10mg/day — requires neurosurgical EVD or reservoir placement; only route achieving adequate CSF concentrations.
• High-dose ampicillin-sulbactam if sulbactam MIC ≤4 (sulbactam has intrinsic CRAB activity).

Shunt removal non-negotiable. Mandatory ID specialist + clinical pharmacist + neurosurgery team consultation.

Skin & Soft Tissue — Case 1 of 2
Non-Purulent Cellulitis · Healthy Host, No Abscess
Cellulitis → Osteomyelitis
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
45-year-old man, healthy, no comorbidities. Two-day spreading erythema, warmth, and tenderness of the right lower leg. No puncture wound, no ulceration, no exudate, no fluctuance on palpation. T 37.8 °C, WBC 12.4 K. No lymphangitic streaking. Erythema borders marked at presentation. No recent antibiotics; no healthcare contact.
Key Clinical Cues — Purulence Determines Pathogen
①
Non-purulent — no abscess, no exudate — IDSA 2014 SSTI classification: non-purulent = GAS-dominant; MRSA coverage not routinely indicated
②
No portal of entry identified — most non-purulent cellulitis enters via microscopic fissures; identify and treat underlying tinea pedis or eczema to prevent recurrence
③
Mark erythema borders at presentation — allows objective 24–48h response assessment; failure to mark is a missed clinical tool
④
Rule out mimics — stasis dermatitis, DVT, lipodermatosclerosis frequently misdiagnosed as cellulitis; consider LENI if unilateral leg edema
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Skin / dermis — non-purulent cellulitis; no deep tissue involvement, no systemic toxicity → outpatient PO therapy appropriate. Admit criteria: failed outpatient Rx, systemic toxicity, immunocompromise, facial cellulitis.
Pathogen
Non-purulent cellulitis → Group A Streptococcus (>70% per IDSA evidence review). S. aureus less common without purulence. CA-MRSA is unlikely in non-purulent cellulitis in a healthy host.
Spectrum
GAS-targeted: cephalexin 500mg QID or dicloxacillin 500mg QID. No MRSA coverage needed for non-purulent cellulitis (IDSA SSTI Guidelines 2014). FQs are spectrum excess here.
PK / PD
Cephalexin achieves adequate dermis/soft-tissue concentrations. β-lactam time-dependent killing: QID dosing (q6h) maintains T>MIC. ~90% oral bioavailability. No IV needed for uncomplicated non-purulent cellulitis.
Host
Healthy host. PCN allergy → clindamycin 300–450mg TID. True PCN anaphylaxis → TMP-SMX (GAS coverage less reliable) or consult. Elevate extremity; treat tinea pedis with topical antifungal.
De-escalate
5-day course if ≥24h improvement at 48h (IDSA). If no improvement at 48–72h → reassess for hidden abscess, DVT, alternative diagnosis, or need for IV admission. Culture not routinely indicated.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If One Variable Changes →
IVDU patient, same leg — now with fluctuant nodule and crusted central eschar
Step 2 (Pathogen) pivots · purulence + IVDU = CA-MRSA until proven otherwise
Purulence changes the dominant pathogen from GAS to S. aureus, specifically CA-MRSA. In IVDU, direct skin inoculation with nasal/skin flora is the mechanism.

Primary intervention: Incision & drainage (I&D) — I&D alone is curative for small uncomplicated abscesses (<2cm); antibiotics are adjunctive, not primary therapy.

Send pus for culture. Add TMP-SMX DS 1–2 tabs BID × 5d or doxycycline 100mg BID × 5d. Cephalexin alone is inadequate for CA-MRSA.

Blood cultures mandatory in febrile IVDU — endocarditis risk is substantial; if bacteremic → minimum 2wk IV and echocardiography.

Skin, Soft Tissue & Bone — Case 2 of 2
Diabetic Foot Osteomyelitis · Polymicrobial, Prior Treatment Failure
Cellulitis → Osteomyelitis
Case Application · Steps 1–6
👤Patient Vignette
64-year-old woman, T2DM (HbA1c 11.2%), peripheral neuropathy, PAD. Chronic right plantar ulcer over 2nd metatarsal × 6 weeks — probe-to-bone positive. Anaerobic odor, purulent discharge. T 38.6 °C, WBC 17.2 K, ESR 94, CRP 148. Plain XR: cortical erosion and periosteal reaction at 2nd metatarsal head. Failed 2-week outpatient amoxicillin-clavulanate. No prior bone cultures available.
Key Clinical Cues — Polymicrobial, Failing Prior Therapy
①
Probe-to-bone positive — PPV ~89% for osteomyelitis; bone biopsy via clean (non-ulcer) skin margin is gold standard and should be obtained before escalating antibiotics
②
Failed amox-clav — β-lactamase inhibitor combination insufficient; do not re-challenge with same class; assume resistant organisms or inadequate spectrum
③
Anaerobic odor + chronic ulcer + T2DM — polymicrobial infection with anaerobes highly probable; GNRs including ESBL possible with chronicity and prior antibiotics
④
HbA1c 11.2% + PAD — poor glycemic control and vascular insufficiency are independent predictors of treatment failure; surgical debridement + glycemic optimization are mandatory adjuncts
🔬Cunha Framework — Step-by-Step
Six-Step Logic Applied to This Case
Site
Bone (osteomyelitis) + deep soft tissue. Bone biopsy via clean skin margin is gold standard — surface swab cultures from ulcer are unreliable and must not guide therapy. Vascular surgery/podiatry/ortho consult for debridement.
Pathogen
Polymicrobial: S. aureus (incl. MRSA) + GNR (E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas if chronic/macerated) + anaerobes (Bacteroides, Peptostreptococcus). Amox-clav failure → MRSA and ESBL GNR now prominent concerns.
Spectrum
Pip-tazo 4.5g q8h IV (polymicrobial, anaerobic coverage) + vancomycin (MRSA). If prior ESBL isolates or ICU-level severity → upgrade to meropenem + vancomycin. Do not re-use amox-clav.
PK / PD
Bone penetration essential. Culture-guided de-escalation to: FQ + rifampin (MSSA/susceptible GNR — excellent bone penetration), clindamycin (GPC/anaerobes), TMP-SMX (MRSA OPAT). Rifampin adds biofilm-penetrating activity — always pair with companion agent; monotherapy rapidly selects resistance.
Host
Check eGFR urgently (DM nephropathy). Vancomycin: AUC-guided, load 25mg/kg × 1. Glycemic optimization (target glucose 140–180 inpatient) essential for wound healing. Vascular surgery evaluation for PAD (revascularization before debridement if indicated). Offloading device required.
De-escalate
Bone biopsy cx drives duration: 2–5 days if infected bone fully resected; 6 weeks if residual infected/devascularized bone remains. OPAT with high-bioavailability PO agent (FQ, linezolid, TMP-SMX) after 1–2wk IV induction.
🔄Circumstance Pivot
If Bone Biopsy Narrows the Diagnosis →
Bone biopsy culture: MSSA only — no GNRs, no anaerobes, no MRSA
Step 3 pivots from polymicrobial empiric → MSSA-targeted monotherapy · all other agents should be stopped
Polymicrobial empiric coverage is no longer justified once the true pathogen is known. Discontinue pip-tazo (or meropenem) and vancomycin entirely.

De-escalate to IV cefazolin 2g q8h (or nafcillin 2g q4h) — both superior to vancomycin for MSSA bone infections (lower treatment failure, better penetration). After 1–2 weeks IV, oral step-down to:
• Dicloxacillin + rifampin, or
• TMP-SMX DS + rifampin (if PCN intolerant).

Complete 6-week total course from last debridement. Rifampin must never be given alone — resistance emerges within days.
